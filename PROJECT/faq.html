Blood Donation FAQs

1. Who can donate blood?

Anyone aged between 18 and 65 years, weighing at least 50 kg (110 lbs), and in good health can donate blood. Some medical conditions may prevent you from donating, so it’s best to check with the donation center.

2. How often can I donate blood?

You can donate whole blood every 56 days (8 weeks). For platelet donation, you can donate every 7 days. Plasma donation can be done every 28 days.

3. What should I do before donating blood?

Before donating, make sure to drink plenty of water and have a light meal. Avoid fatty foods and alcohol the day before your donation.

4. What happens during the donation process?

The process usually takes about 30 minutes. After checking your health, you'll sit in a comfortable chair while a healthcare professional collects the blood through a sterile needle. The actual donation lasts about 10 minutes.

5. Is donating blood safe?

Yes, donating blood is safe. All equipment used is sterile and disposable. There are very few side effects, and most people feel fine after donating.

6. How will I feel after donating blood?

Most people feel fine after donating. However, you might feel a little tired. It’s important to rest for a few minutes and have a snack and drink to replenish your energy.

7. Can I donate blood if I’m taking medication?

It depends on the medication. Some medications may affect your eligibility to donate blood. Always check with the blood donation center beforehand.

8. How is my blood used after donation?

Your blood can be used for patients undergoing surgery, those with chronic illnesses, or those who have experienced trauma. It can be separated into components (red cells, platelets, plasma) to treat different medical conditions.
